\begin{abstract}
\noindent This paper analyses the differences between how AI and humans react to unexpected situations that force a reliance on past experiences, creative leaps, and guesswork. Specifically, when neural networks, Large Language Models, and humans are tasked with classifying images into classes that poorly or do not at all reflect the contents of the images. It found that the fact that neural networks lack the ability to make creative leaps or guesses holds it back greatly from being able to process unexpected data in a meaningful or effective way. It also found that Large Language Models are able to recognise when creativity is needed to solve a problem, but are not always able to make the leaps in logic that is required to form creative solutions, and instead rely a bit too often on guesswork.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\subsection{Background}
The human brain is an incredibly complicated example of biological machinery. It can store information and apply it to a variety of situations to efficiently solve problems, complete tasks, understand the world around them, and adapt to change. This allows humans to not only learn from a guide, but from their own mistakes and past experiences.

Neural networks are a subcategory of AI that attempts to recreate this behaviour using code, calculus and matrices. Programs are great at following instructions to get from an expected input to an expected output. However, in the real world, not every piece of data can be anticipated.

This research aims to find the similarities and difference between how humans, neural networks, and Large Language Models react to unexpected inputs through an image classification task with limited class options.
\subsection{Neural Networks}
\begin{figure}[hb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\linewidth]{Images/neuralNet.jpg}
    \caption{A diagram of a neural network with four layers of nodes. $\Sigma$ represents the summation function and $f$ represents the activation function. Taken from \url{https://www.knime.com/blog/a-friendly-introduction-to-deep-neural-networks}}
    \label{fig:netimage}
\end{figure}
\noindent Neural networks are a branch of AI that uses algorithms to mimic the structure of neurons in the human brain. 

They consist of layers of nodes that can receive multiple numerical inputs and perform mathematical functions on them [Fig. \ref{fig:netimage}]. Each node applies different weights to each of its inputs, sums them, add a bias, then performs a non-linear activation function to allow the network to learn non-linear relationships. The weights and biases determine how much each node can affect the final output, and is what changes as the network learns.

The way the network decides the weight of a node is based on its learning paradigm. The paradigm used in this research is supervised learning with backpropagation, but other paradigms such as unsupervised or reinforcement learning exist \cite{lampropoulos2015machine}.

Supervised learning with backpropagation is where inputs come paired with an expected output, and the network aims to get results as close to the expected output as possible. It does this by calculating a loss function to measure the difference between its outputs and the expected outputs, using calculus' chain rule to find the minimum point of this function, and updating the weights and biases to get closer to this value.


\subsection{Image Recognition}
Image recognition is an application of neural networks where images are classified into categories based on the contents of the image.
\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.9\linewidth]{Images/supervisedImages.png}
    \caption{An example of images from a supervised dataset, with images on the left and labels for the images on the right.}
    \label{fig:supimages}
\end{figure}

Supervised learning is a common paradigm for image recognition \cite{krizhevsky2012imagenet}. This is because images come with labels, which are the expected outputs used by the algorithm. Some examples of images with labels can be seen in [Fig. \ref{fig:supimages}]. This suits image recognition as, during training, the neural network can generalise the links between characteristics it finds within the image and the given labels by changing its nodes' weights and biases, and use these generalisations to make predictions.


\subsection{Large Language Models}
Large Language Models (LLMs) are a subsection of Artificial Intelligence that have been trained on vast amounts of data in order to process and generate natural language \cite{bommasani2022opportunitiesrisksfoundationmodels}. Common examples include OpenAI's ChatGPT \cite{ChatGPT}, Google's Gemini \cite{Gemini}, and Microsoft's Copilot \cite{Copilot}.


\subsection{Zero-Shot Inference}
Zero-shot inference is where a neural network is given a task it has not been explicitly trained to do \cite{mehta2025generalizationtheoryzeroshotprediction}. In image classification, this means forcing the network to classify images into categories it has not been specifically trained to identify.

As LLMs are trained on vast amounts of data, it is unlikely that the network is completely unfamiliar with the categories it is given. Here, "zero-shot" refers to providing the LLM with a prompt that contains no examples for what fits into each category, nor does it include descriptions of features to look out for. It is usually only given the category names, and the images to classify. This means that the LLM must create its own method for classifying the images.


\section{Literature Review}
\begin{figure}[hb]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Images/geirhosDistorts.png}
    \caption{Example images used by Geirhos et al. \cite{geirhos2018generalisation} of a bird across all distortions types used. From left to right, image manipulations are: colour (undistorted), greyscale, low contrast, high-pass, low-pass (blurring), phase noise, power equalisation. Bottom row: opponent colour, rotation, Eidolon I, II and III, additive uniform noise, salt-and-pepper noise.}
    \label{fig:geirhosDistorts}
\end{figure}
\noindent\textbf{Generalisation in humans and deep neural networks \cite{geirhos2018generalisation}} --
Geirhos et al. aimed to compare the success rate of humans against deep neural networks when tasked with recognising distorted images. These distortions mainly focussed on changing the colours or amount of noise in the image [Fig. \ref{fig:geirhosDistorts}]. They found that humans were mostly more accurate on average at identifying distorted images than the networks for non-colour related distortions, where the networks always had a higher average accuracy. They also found that human participants’ responses were distributed almost entirely equally, whereas the networks showed increasing biases towards certain categories as the distortions got stronger.

I have included 2 distortions that were not used in this study: kaleidoscope and liquefy. These distortions, unlike the distortions used by Geirhos et al., change the shape of the object in the image.

\subsection{Creating a Neural Network}
\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{Images/convolution.png}
    \caption{The process of convolution. The input image represents the pixels in the image, or the output array of previous iterations. The output array is another term for a feature map. Source: \url{https://www.ibm.com/think/topics/convolutional-neural-networks}}
    \label{fig:convolution}
\end{figure}
I created a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) in Python using TensorFlow's Keras library and trained it on CIFAR-10's training dataset \cite{krizhevsky2009learning}. When an image is inputted into it, it outputs the most likely class, and a confidence score as a percentage. The layout is as follows:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Convolution layer [Fig. \ref{fig:convolution}] -- The image is split into groups of pixels, 3x3 in size, stored as matrices. A dot product is calculated between each matrix of pixels and a filter. A filter is a matrix that tests for the likelihood of a certain feature being present in an image. These filters change as the CNN learns. These dot products then come together to form a feature map, which shows where the feature the filter was testing for is in the image.
    \item ReLU -- An activation function that executes immediately after convolution. For each value in the feature map, negative values become 0 and positive values remain constant. It allows the CNN to learn non-linear as well as linear relationships between feature and classification. 
    \item Max pooling layer -- Reduces dimensions on the feature map by converting a group of 2x2 pixels into one pixel that takes the greatest value within the group.
    \item Each of the above is repeated twice more, for three of each.
    \item Fully-connected layer -- Uses a softmax function to give a probability of how likely each feature corresponds to each category.
\end{itemize}

I have taken two precautions to prevent overfitting (where the CNN is too accurate with the training data, such that it is very inaccurate with the unseen testing data). One is data augmentation, which is where small, random adjustments are applied to the images before training \cite{shorten2019survey}. The other is dropout (at a rate of 0.5), which is where nodes are randomly disabled to prevent the CNN from focussing to closely on certain features and enable the CNN to make more general and robust links between feature and classification \cite{srivastava2014dropout}.


I used Python because it is the language I am most experienced with, and it is the most popular language for machine learning, thanks to its machine learning libraries such as TensorFlow, Scikit-learn, PyTorch, and Keras \cite{overflow2022libraries,newhorizons2023pythonML}.


I used Keras because of its extensive documentation that guided me through every step \cite{kerasDocs}. Its syntax was also the most intuitive for me out of the most popular libraries, so I was able to get started with coding faster.


I chose to create a Convolutional Neural Network because the convolutional layer, that is exclusive to CNNs, lends itself well to image recognition as it mimics the human process of looking for recognisable features \cite{rawat2017CNN}.

\subsection{Retraining Publicly Available Neural Networks}
I have retrained the VGG16 \cite{simonyan2015deepconvolutionalnetworkslargescale}, ConvNext \cite{liu2022convnet2020s}, and CLIP \cite{radford2021learningtransferablevisualmodels} neural networks using transfer learning. They were originally trained on ImageNet \cite{ImageNet}, an image dataset with over 14 million images and 100,000 categories.

Transfer learning is where only the final layer of the neural network is changed from its initial training, to after it has been retrained. This means that most of the nodes, weights, and functions stay the same, but the final output is changed. Int his case, this means that instead being able to classify images into one of 100,00 categories, it can only classify into the 10 CIFAR-10 categories with very little training needing to be done.

I chose these neural networks because they were the only models in Keras' library that supported 32x32 images, which CIFAR-10 images are.


\subsection{Using Zero-Shot Inference with Large Language Models}
I provided 4 web-based Large Language Models (ChatGPT \cite{ChatGPT}, Gemini \cite{Gemini}, Copilot \cite{Copilot}, and Claude \cite{Claude}) with the following prompt and attached the images alongside it.
\begin{quote}
    You will be given 10 images, and you must classify these images into a category based on the contents of the image. These categories are: airplane, automobile, bird, cat, deer, dog, frog, horse, ship, truck. For some images, none of these categories may seem suitable, but you must select from one of these categories, and give a reason behind your choice. Please also rate how well your chosen category reflects the contents of the image, between 1 and 10 inclusive. The images are attached.
\end{quote}

\subsection{Survey}
I created a survey using Google Forms and sent it out via friends of friends and online survey swapping websites to 71 people, with 69 of them answering the validation question correctly. The respondents were asked the same three questions for each of the 10 images, with the images being in the order shown above [Fig. \ref{fig:testImages}]. The questions and multiple-choice options were as follows.
\begin{enumerate}
    \item What category would you select for this image? \\ Options: Airplane; Automobile; Bird; Cat; Deer; Dog; Frog; Horse; Ship; Truck.
    \item Why did you pick that category? \\ Options: It fit the image the best; Random choice; Just chose the first/last/other option; Drawn to that category; Don't know; Other (Custom responses).
    \item How well does the category you chose fit the image, in your opinion? \\ Options: Integers 1 through 10.
\end{enumerate}

Even for each image, the choices were very spread out. Every image had at least 6 different categories chosen by somebody, except for train [Fig. \ref{fig:train}] with 3 categories, and of course pigeon [Fig. \ref{fig:pigeon}].

The answer to why this is lies in the reasons given for each response. Some notable responses behind the graph on the left [Fig. \ref{fig:catssandwich}] include: "Food gives you energy, energy makes you move, automobiles move", "Can look at it as something served on a ship as food", and "I pictured this as a horse burger". These responses show how the least-chosen classes where chosen based on creative and unusual reasoning that other people did not consider.

Despite this, most responses mentioned making the choice purely based off of chance. Especially the dog category, where the majority of the respondents who categorised the sandwich as a dog said the choice was purely random. I, however, don't believe the choice to pick dog was by pure random chance, given that it was the tied first choice option, with the majority of the other options having less than half of the responses. Whether this did happen by chance, or because respondents were unwilling to share their actual reasons, or because of some subconscious bias, or some other reason; I am not sure.

\subsection{Neural Networks}
Neural networks can only use feature extraction from the images to categorise the image, not use any creativity or unusual reasoning. Airplanes and automobiles are man-made objects with no particular set colour, shape, or size. This means that it is likely that the neural networks have learned of many different features that could correspond to airplanes or cars, rather than more specific features that other categories may have, causing the neural networks to have a bias towards those categories when the specific features of the other categories are not present.

\vspace{3mm}
Therefore, the fact that neural networks lack the means to effectively handle unexpected data as they are unable to deviate from their learnt functions and be creative -- like humans can -- forces the network to instead rely on biases learned from the training data. This causes unsuitable and meaningless results that do not reflect the results a human produces with the same data.



\subsection{Large Language Models}
The Large Language Models were similarly spread out across the categories as the humans were, but the reasons showed this was for quite a different reason.

While people seemed split between either creative logic or random choices, the LLMs seemed split between either feature extraction or random choices, only occasionally being able to produce complex reasons behind categories. This presents LLMs as a halfway point between neural networks and humans in terms of creativity and complex thought.

Some LLMs performed better than others. For example, ChatGPT was able to recognise that feature extraction was not a good enough technique for this task, but was not able to create creative techniques, instead relying on random choices for almost every image. This is in contrast to Google's Gemini, which employed more human-like thought processes. One example is for the abstract painting [Fig. \ref{fig:abstract}]: "The bright colors and layered texture are reminiscent of the vibrant feathers found on tropical birds.". This shows that LLMs have potential for more complex and creative solutions to problems, but are not currently at a human level.

\vspace{3mm}
\noindent Therefore, LLMs have the training to be able to recognise when it is faced with unexpected data and that a different technique to what it knows needs to be employed. However, utilising creative and complex solutions came with varying degrees of success for different models on different images. This places LLMs firmly between the low bar of neural networks and the high bar of human ability.


\section{Conclusion}
This research compared how neural networks and humans react to the unexpected through observing the patterns that form when tasked with classifying images into unsuitable categories. The results showed that neural networks do not react to unexpected data in a similar fashion to humans. Neural networks are only able to apply the same functions to all inputs, regardless of whether it is expected or not. On the other hand, humans are able to change their approach by applying creativity to their thought process, such as using outside knowledge about the task to inform their decisions. This causes the neural networks to produce meaningless results which do not reflect the inputted data.

The results also showed that Large Language Models have the potential to employ creative techniques to solve unexpected problems. They are quite often able to recognise when more creative techniques need to be employed over simple ones, but are less often able to actually employ one. Instead, in these cases, they turn to arbitrary and random solutions to prevent themselves from straying too far from logic.

\vspace{3mm}
\noindent This research is important because it demonstrates that training/education is vital for both AI and humans in order to ensure that they are well-equipped to handle the situations/prompts they are given and aren't left guessing like they were forced to in this study. 

This research also shows how important the ability to creatively create new logic is when the objectively best approach to a problem cannot be achieved in the situation at hand.

\vspace{3mm}
\noindent This research could be extended with out of distribution detection, a process that is able to identify when inputs are not expected by the neural network. This would give the neural networks an opportunity to recognise when it is not able to handle the given task, and the user an opportunity to change their approach or implement different approaches into the neural network.
